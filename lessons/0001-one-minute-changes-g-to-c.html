One Minute Changes

Make one chord change clean, fast, and automatic — then rotate to the next.

How to use this drill. Pick one chord pair and drill only that pair — isolating a single change is what builds the muscle memory; mixing chords randomly is slower and more frustrating. Stay on a pair until it's solid, then rotate to the next. Start with G ⇄ C: it's everywhere in songs and forces a big hand reposition, so cracking it makes the rest easier. The one idea: minimal movement

  1. Find the pivot. For most pairs, one finger barely moves or a shape just shifts. Spot what stays and move as little as possible — don't lift the whole hand off the neck.
  2. Move as a unit. Aim all fingers at the new shape and land them together, not one at a time.
  3. Air changes. Lift just off the strings and switch shapes in the air, with no pressing. Train the shape first, the pressing second.
  4. Clean > fast. Press, strum once, check every note rings. Only speed up when it's clean. Never drill a buzz.

Best pace held clean: —

Start at the pace you can hold clean. When ~10 changes in a row feel easy, nudge +2 and tap “held it” to bank it (saved per pair). Drop back the moment notes buzz. Rest your hand between rounds.

Which pair next? (the rotation)

Master one pair before adding the next; each day, warm up the ones you know, then spend most time on your weakest pair. A high-value order:
  • G⇄C → G⇄D → C⇄D. These three are the I–IV–V of G major — the most common progression in Western music, and the key to hundreds of songs.
  • Add Em: G⇄Em, C⇄Em, D⇄Em (Em is the easiest open chord — a quick win).
  • Add Am: C⇄Am, G⇄Am, D⇄Am.
Don't randomise the drill — the “random” practice that matters is playing real progressions, which is exactly what Lesson 2 trains. Chord/progression picks: Fender — Essential Beginner Chords G, C, D · common beginner progressions.

Make it music (≈4 min)

Count a slow 1–2–3–4 out loud. Strum the first chord on beat 1, let 2–3–4 ring while you move to the second, strum it on the next beat 1. The goal isn't speed — it's changing in time without stopping the count.
