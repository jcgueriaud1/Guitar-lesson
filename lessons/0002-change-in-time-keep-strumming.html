Change in Time — Keep the Strum Moving

Change chords without ever stopping your strumming hand.

Why this is the bridge. In Lesson 1 you changed G⇄C on your own clock. A song doesn't wait. The single skill that turns drills into music is this: your strumming hand keeps moving like a pendulum and never pauses — even when your fretting hand isn't ready yet. Stop the strum to "fix" a chord and you've stopped the song. Keep it moving and you'll play in time long before every change is perfect.

The one idea: never stop the pendulum

  1. The hand is a metronome. Your strumming arm swings down–up–down–up continuously, in time, whether or not it hits the strings. It does not pause for the chord change.
  2. Change on the way. Lift your fretting fingers and move to the next shape during the last up-strum of the bar — a hair early — so they land as the next bar begins.
  3. If you're not ready, strum anyway. Fingers not down yet? Let the strum brush muted strings and catch up on the next beat. A muffled strum in time beats a clean strum that's late.
  4. Slow enough to win. Set a tempo where you make the change every single bar without stopping. Only then nudge it up.

The trainer — hands-free, in time (≈8 min)

G
 
60beats / min

Hit Start, then strum along. Bold arrows = strum the strings; faded arrows = your hand still swings but misses. The chord flips each bar — watch for “get ready →” on beat 4.

Golden rule of this lesson: if you have to choose between a clean chord and a steady strum — keep the strum steady. Rhythm is the skeleton of music; a slightly buzzy chord in time still sounds like a song, a perfect chord out of time does not.

Make it music (≈3 min)

Pick a slow tempo on "Down on every beat" and play two bars of G, two bars of C, round and round, never stopping the hand. That four-bar loop is a real chord progression you'll meet in dozens of songs. When it feels easy, switch to Old Faithful at the same tempo — same job, more groove.
